About Kashi Matha

Kashi Math is a matha (monastery) and a spiritual organisation followed by the Madhva section of Gaud Saraswat Brahmins, who are also referred as Madhwa Saraswat Brahmins or Vaishnava Saraswat Brahmins. It dates back to the 16th century. With its headquarters in Brahma Ghat, Varanasi. Kashi Math has followers all over the Konkan belt, prominently in Mumbai, Goa, Udupi, Mangalore, Kochi and other parts of Karnataka and Kerala.

Deities

The principal deities of Kashi Math are charaprathishta ("moving installation") idols of Vyasa, Rama and Narasimha, who are also collectively known as the Vyasa Raghupathi Narasimha.

Gurus

The Kashi Math follows the Guru-shishya tradition where the Guru of the Math initiates a shishya to succeed him upon his Samadhi. Samyamindra Thirtha is the 21st and current head (Mathadipathi) of the Math. The heads have been:

  • Yadavendra Thirtha - I
  • Keshavendra Thirtha
  • Upendra Thirtha - I
  • Yadavendra Thirtha - II
  • Raghavendra Thirtha
  • Devendra Thirtha
  • Madhavendra Thirtha
  • Jnaneendra Thirtha
  • Yadavendra Thirtha - III
  • Upendra Thirtha - II
  • Rajendra Thirtha
  • Sureendra Thirtha
  • Vishnu Thirtha
  • Vibhudendra Thirtha

About Bantwal Kashi Math

The Kashimut at Bantwal, a branch of the main Kashimut in Varanasi, is a serene and spiritually significant site, surrounded by a lush garden-like environment. This matta holds great historical and religious importance, particularly for the GSB (Gowda Saraswath Brahmin) community.

The matta is closely associated with *Deevendra Teertha Swamiji, the 6th Swamiji of Kashimut, who spent most of his exile in Bantwal, residing at the Venkatramana Temple. After his demise, his Samadhi was established here, though his body was buried on the banks of the *Netravati River*, where annual poojas are performed to honor him. The matta was built under his guidance, making it a vital spiritual center.

The temple is renowned for its Hanuman idol, which holds a unique place among all Kashimut mattas. Unlike others, this matta features a large, ferocious form of Hanuman, with his left leg forward, symbolizing anger and power. To calm this ferocious energy, water is continuously dripped over the idol, much like the ritual performed for a Shivalinga. This Hanuman idol is believed to have immense spiritual power, and devotees from across India visit to seek blessings and protection from bad omens.

Apart from the GSB community, the temple attracts people of all backgrounds who come to experience its serene atmosphere and spiritual significance. The annual rituals and poojas performed here enhance its sanctity, making it a major center of devotion and cultural heritage in Bantwal.
